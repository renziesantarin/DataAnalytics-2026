1. Start by reviewing the data.gov home page. What is this?
- Data.gov is a public website that started on January 21, 2009. It provides access to a variety of subjects including transportation, agriculture, education, and health that are published by the U.S. government.

2. Go to Most Viewed Datasets. How many datasets are listed?
- As of today, 04/07/2026, there are 402,363 datasets found in the most viewed datasets section.

3. Find the Topic Category for Human Health. Review the range of data sources
available for this topic. Why do you think these were tagged with the Human Health
category?
- Majority of the data sources were about food, clean air status, or anything that might cause a medical emergency. They were tagged with Human Health category because they have information related to public health, healthcare access, nutrition as it is related to overall health, disease or medical emergency related statistics.

4. Go to the Food Price Outlook. Review the catalog page for this dataset. How can you
navigate to additional datasets from the USDA?
- Click the organization name, it will take you to the USDA page which will have all the associated datasets.

5. Go back to the data.gov home page. This time, click on Datasets by Organization.
- Done

6. Try searching for your city, county, and state. What organizations are returned?
- When I searched for Newark or Delaware no organizations were found.

7. Search for USDA, and click into the result. Within the USDA datasets, search for food
composition. Notice that the top three results are all datasets concerning nutrition
information. Try searching the USDA page for nutrition. How does that affect your
results?
- The results of searching for "nutrition" were more closely related than those for "food composition." This demonstrates how search terms can have a big impact on the datasets that you will see.

8. Go back to the results for food composition, and click on FoodData Central. Review
the catalog page for this data source, then click on the button for Visit Page. Take
some time to explore the FoodData Central website.
a) Try searching for butter, or any of your favorite foods. What kind of results do you
get? What happens when you click on a result?
- There were several listings available with different brands and different types of butter. When I clicked on one of the result, it showed a comprehensive list with nutritional value, serving size, and there are other details such as sample size, what year the sample was acquired and from where.

b) Go back to the home page. Find Foundation Foods, and click on Downloads. What
kind of data can you find on this page? How big are these files? Where can you find
more information on the data types available?
- On the Downloads page, there are JSON and CSV zipped files. The size of the files depends on the dataset version and type. In the documentation part, you'll see additional information and resources to understand the data types and format. You can also download a file that contains information regarding the definition of all terms and labels used in the files. If you scroll to the bottom of the page, you'll also see information about historical download and releases.
